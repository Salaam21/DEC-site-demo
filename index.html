About DEC

The Drug Enforcement Commission (DEC) was established in 1989 by an Act of Parliament. DEC operates under The Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act Number 35 of 2021 of the laws of Zambia, and the Prohibition and Prevention of Money Laundering ACT no. 14 of 2001 as amended.

History

Dr. Kaunda played a critical role in curbing the problem of drug trafficking which involved Zambian Air Hostesses from Zambia Airways dating as far back as the early 1980s. The Air Hostesses were arrested for drug trafficking into the United Kingdom at Heathrow Airport and this led to the British Government tasking its Law Enforcement Agencies to jointly work with the Zambian Government in establishing the source of the drugs. Back home, Dr. Kaunda did not just investigate the case at hand but further constituted a Commission of Inquiry to inter alia establish the extent of the problem of drug trafficking and money laundering in Zambia. The Justice Matthews Chaila chaired Commission concluded its inquiry and in 1985 submitted its report to the Republican President.

Among the findings of the Chaila Commission of Inquiry was that several people of Zambian and foreign origin were engaging themselves in drug trafficking and money laundering activities. These included some Government officials who were using their Diplomatic Passports to evade security searches at points of entry into the country to evade searches by law enforcement officers.

Being the "Man of Action" that he was, Dr. Kaunda immediatelysetup the Special Investigations Team on Economy and Trade (SITET) following the recommendations of the Chaila Commission. The Team which was housed at DEC's current Headquarters' building had the mandate of investigating economic crimes and ensured that the Republic of Zambia's economy was protected from criminals. To deal with the problem of drug trafficking, the then President of Zambia ordered for a combined team of security forces to mount "Clean Up" operations in Lusaka in which random searches were being conducted on suspects. The operations led to the arrest of many suspects who included serving Ministers at the time.

In 1989, the Government of the Republic of Zambia under the leadership of Dr. Kaunda amended the Dangerous Drugs Act of 1967 to include recommendations from the Chaila Commission of Inquiry and provide for the formation of the Drug Enforcement Commission. This was a permanent body which was to investigate drug and money laundering related offenses.

From the time DEC was formed, President Kaunda and all Presidents that came into power afterwards, have always supported the work of the Commission and offered counsel to all the Commissioners.

Amendment of Acts

The Acts were later amended in 2021. The Commission is now operating under the following:

  • Act No. 33 of 2021 Cannabis.
  • Act No. 35 of 2021 of the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances.
  • Act No. 34 of 2021 of The Industrial Hemp.
  • The Acts 14 and 44 Prohibition and Prevention of Money Laundering.

Vision

A reliable, proactive and independent Commission.

Mission Statement

To enforce the law on narcotic drugs, psychotropic substances and money laundering for a safe and conducive socio-economic environment. Providing rehabilitation services to drug addicts, to contribute to socio-economic development and maintenance of national security.

Values

  • Impartiality: We shall execute our duties in an ethical manner, according to the prescribed standards of the Commission.
  • Integrity: We shall uphold moral uprightness and put our obligations to the nation above our personal interests in the execution of our duties.
  • Teamwork: We shall collaborate to achieve a common goal in the most efficient and effective manner.
  • Innovativeness: In the execution of our duties, we shall apply new ideas and methods for better solutions.
  • Confidentiality: We shall be trustworthy by not revealing privileged information to unauthorized persons.
  • Accountability: We shall be answerable and responsible for our actions, omissions and decisions in a transparent manner.

Operational Departments

The Drug Enforcement Commission is composed of three (03) Departments namely Drug Supply Reduction, Drug Demand Reduction and Anti-Money Laundering Investigations Unit.

Drug Supply Reduction

This is a unit that effects seizures and arrests of people involved in illicit drug trafficking and all prohibited drug related substances respectively. The following are the divisions that make up the Supply Reduction Department:

  • Investigation
  • Intelligence
  • Canine Section
  • Prosecutions

Drug Demand Reduction

This is the Unit with the responsibility of creating awareness on the dangers of abusing drugs and substances of the health of our citizens. To effectively carry out the above mandate, the commission established the Education Counselling Department (ECD). The Education and Counselling Division (ECD) champions this division and has got drug abuse preventive or sensitization programmes as follows:

  • Institutions of Learning: (Kindergarten, Pre-schools, Primary, High Schools, colleges, and Universities). Institutions of learning programme are a drug preventive programme that deals with kindergarten, Pre- Schools, Primary, High Schools, Colleges, and Universities. Its main objective is to enlighten pupils and students on the dangers of abusing drugs and alcohol.
  • Community and Youth Mobilization: (Churches, Faith Based Organisations (FBO'S), Community Based Organisations (CBO'S), Football Teams, Youth Organisations etc.) This is one of the preventive programmes that deal with educating the community on the dangers of illegal and legal substances. It helps to educate on the awareness of Narcotic drugs and Psychotropic substances as well as money laundering to contribute to the socio-economic development. Its main Objective is to Sensitize the public on Narcotic drugs and Psychotropic substances and Money laundering. The following are some of the ways used to disseminate information to the public:
    • Use of Drug kits: This is done to show the local public what drugs look like.
    • Use of Aids
    • Posters
    • Use of Drama
    • Songs
    • Sketches
  • Workplace Programmes: Workplace programme is also a preventive programme that involves going to workplaces such as, Banks, Hotels, Industries, lodges etc. for information dissemination on drugs.
  • Counselling and Rehabilitation Services: Counselling is a therapeutic relationship between a qualified counsellor and the drug dependent. Drug counselling and rehabilitation is a term for the process of medical or psychotherapeutic treatment, for the dependency on psychoactive substances such as alcohol, prescription drugs and illicit drugs such as cocaine, heroin, amphetamines, or marijuana. The general intent is to enable the patient to cease substance abuse to avoid the psychological, legal, financial social and physical consequences that can be caused, especially by extreme abuse. Therefore, counselling and rehabilitation is one of the preventive programmes that are offered at Education and Counselling Department. Its main aim is to offer counselling to drug abusers.

Aims and Objectives of the Education and Counselling Division

  • To disseminate information to the public through drug sensitizations and information dissemination through all forms of media and offers free counselling services.
  • To offer free services to the Zambian citizens and foreigners.
  • To carry out drug demand reduction activities to the public which include educating and sensitizing the public on the evils and dangerous effects of trafficking in and abusing illicit drugs.
  • To initiate and implement programmes that will ultimately reduce the demand for illicit drugs by members of the public.
  • The Commission also recognises that the drug scourge leaves behind casualties in form of dependents.
  • To offer counselling and rehabilitation services that alleviates drug dependants from their suffering and endeavour to fit them back into society.

Drugs

Cocaine

Category: Stimulant

Effects: Cocaine is a powerful stimulant that significantly heightens alertness, increases energy levels, and induces a strong sense of euphoria. However, prolonged use can lead to severe paranoia, hallucinations, and irregular heart rhythms. Users may also experience anxiety, restlessness, and irritability.

Composition: Cocaine is derived from the leaves of the coca plant, primarily found in South America. The drug is often adulterated with harmful additives such as talcum powder, cornstarch, or anesthetics like lidocaine to increase its volume and profitability. These additives can introduce additional health risks.

Risks: Cocaine is highly addictive, leading to physical and psychological dependence. Long-term use can result in severe health issues, including stroke, respiratory failure, and severe mental health problems such as depression and anxiety. Overdoses are common and can be fatal, often resulting in heart attacks or seizures. Chronic use can also lead to significant weight loss, malnutrition, and damage to nasal tissues if snorted.

Heroin

Category: Opioid

Effects: Heroin produces a sense of intense euphoria and pain relief by binding to opioid receptors in the brain. However, it also depresses the central nervous system, leading to slowed breathing and heart rate. In cases of overdose, this can result in respiratory arrest and death. Users may also experience drowsiness, clouded mental functioning, and nausea.

Composition: Heroin is synthesized from morphine, which is extracted from the opium poppy plant. It is often mixed with other substances like fentanyl, a synthetic opioid that is significantly more potent than heroin, increasing its potency and danger. Other common adulterants include sugar, starch, powdered milk, and quinine, which can introduce additional health risks and complications.

Risks: Heroin is extremely addictive, with a high risk of both physical and psychological dependence. Long-term use can lead to severe health issues, including collapsed veins, abscesses, and infections of the heart lining and valves. Sharing needles increases the risk of contracting infectious diseases such as HIV and hepatitis. Chronic use can also cause liver and kidney disease, as well as severe constipation and gastrointestinal problems. Overdoses are common and can be fatal, often resulting in respiratory failure or cardiac arrest.

Marijuana

Category: Psychoactive

Effects: Marijuana alters perception and induces relaxation by interacting with the brain's endocannabinoid system. It can enhance sensory experiences and create a sense of euphoria. However, high doses may cause hallucinations, increased anxiety, and paranoia. Users might also experience dry mouth, red eyes, and impaired memory and coordination.

Composition: Marijuana contains THC (tetrahydrocannabinol), the primary psychoactive component responsible for its mind-altering effects. It also contains CBD (cannabidiol), which has potential therapeutic effects such as reducing inflammation and anxiety without causing a high. The plant contains over 100 other cannabinoids, as well as terpenes and flavonoids, which contribute to its aroma and potential health benefits.

Risks: Marijuana use can impact brain development in young users, leading to long-term cognitive impairments. It can reduce cognitive functions such as attention, memory, and learning. Chronic use may trigger mental health disorders like psychosis, especially in individuals with a predisposition to such conditions. Additionally, smoking marijuana can harm lung health, similar to tobacco smoke, and may lead to chronic bronchitis and other respiratory issues.

Methamphetamine

Category: Stimulant

Effects: Methamphetamine increases energy, suppresses appetite, and induces a false sense of well-being by stimulating the release of dopamine in the brain. Prolonged use can cause severe dental decay, commonly known as "meth mouth," due to dry mouth and poor oral hygiene. Users may also experience paranoia, hallucinations, and violent behavior. Other effects include increased heart rate, blood pressure, and body temperature.

Composition: Methamphetamine is a synthetic drug made using ephedrine or pseudoephedrine, which are common ingredients in over-the-counter cold medications. These are combined with toxic chemicals such as ammonia, lithium, red phosphorus, and hydrochloric acid during the manufacturing process. The resulting product is often contaminated with dangerous byproducts and impurities.

Risks: Methamphetamine is highly addictive, leading to both physical and psychological dependence. Long-term use can cause significant brain damage, affecting memory, judgment, and motor skills. It also increases the risk of cardiovascular issues, such as heart attacks and strokes. Chronic use can result in irreversible organ damage, including liver and kidney failure. Additionally, users are at a higher risk of contracting infectious diseases like HIV and hepatitis due to needle sharing and risky behaviors.

Alcohol

Category: Depressant

Effects: Alcohol reduces inhibition, impairs coordination, and affects decision-making abilities by depressing the central nervous system. It can create a sense of relaxation and euphoria in small amounts, but higher doses lead to slurred speech, impaired motor skills, and memory blackouts. Chronic use can damage the liver and nervous system, leading to conditions such as alcoholic hepatitis, cirrhosis, and neuropathy.

Composition: Alcohol contains ethanol, a psychoactive substance produced by the fermentation of sugars found in fruits, grains, and other plants. The fermentation process involves yeast or bacteria converting the sugars into ethanol and carbon dioxide. Different types of alcoholic beverages, such as beer, wine, and spirits, vary in their ethanol content and the ingredients used in their production.

Risks: Dependency on alcohol can lead to alcoholism, a chronic disease characterized by an inability to control drinking despite negative consequences. Long-term use increases the risk of liver cirrhosis, a severe scarring of the liver tissue that impairs its function. Alcohol abuse can also cause brain damage, leading to cognitive impairments and mental health disorders. Social issues such as violence, accidents, and relationship problems are common among individuals with alcohol dependency.

Inhalants

Category: Psychoactive

Effects: Inhalants cause rapid intoxication, leading to dizziness, slurred speech, and hallucinations by depressing the central nervous system. Users may also experience euphoria, lightheadedness, and impaired judgment. Chronic use can cause severe nerve and brain damage, resulting in cognitive impairments, memory loss, and motor skill deficits. Other effects include headaches, nausea, and muscle weakness.

Composition: Inhalants include a variety of volatile substances such as glue, paint thinners, nitrous oxide, gasoline, and aerosol sprays. These substances contain chemicals like toluene, acetone, and butane, which produce psychoactive effects when inhaled. The composition of inhalants can vary widely, making them unpredictable and dangerous.

Risks: Inhalants carry a high potential for sudden death due to heart failure, a phenomenon known as "sudden sniffing death syndrome." Long-term use can lead to irreversible damage to the brain and liver, as well as other organs. Chronic inhalant abuse can cause respiratory issues, hearing loss, and bone marrow damage. Additionally, users are at risk of accidents and injuries due to impaired coordination and judgment while under the influence.

Ecstasy (MDMA)

Category: Psychoactive

Effects: Ecstasy (MDMA) induces heightened sensations, emotional warmth, and increased energy by releasing large amounts of serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine in the brain. Users often experience enhanced sensory perception, feelings of closeness, and empathy. However, it can also cause dehydration, hyperthermia (dangerously high body temperature), and serotonin syndrome, a potentially life-threatening condition characterized by high levels of serotonin in the brain. Other effects include jaw clenching, teeth grinding, and blurred vision.

Composition: Ecstasy is a synthetic drug that combines stimulant and hallucinogenic properties. It is chemically similar to both amphetamines and mescaline. The drug is often produced in clandestine laboratories and may be adulterated with other substances such as caffeine, methamphetamine, or synthetic cathinones ("bath salts"), which can increase its potency and danger.

Risks: Ecstasy carries a risk of organ failure, particularly liver and kidney damage, due to its toxic effects on these organs. Long-term use can lead to memory loss, cognitive impairments, and severe mood swings. Chronic use depletes serotonin levels in the brain, leading to depression, anxiety, and other mental health issues. Additionally, the drug's stimulant effects can strain the cardiovascular system, increasing the risk of heart attack or stroke.

LSD

Category: Hallucinogen

Effects: LSD (Lysergic acid diethylamide) causes vivid hallucinations, altered perception of time, and intense emotional swings by interacting with serotonin receptors in the brain. Users may experience synesthesia, where senses overlap, such as "seeing" sounds or "hearing" colors. The effects can last up to 12 hours, and users might also experience increased heart rate, elevated blood pressure, and dilated pupils. Flashbacks, or hallucinogen persisting perception disorder (HPPD), can occur even after stopping use, causing distressing visual disturbances.

Composition: LSD is synthesized from lysergic acid, a compound found in ergot fungus that grows on rye and other grains. The drug is typically distributed in the form of blotter paper, tablets, or liquid, and is often ingested orally. Due to its potency, only small doses are required to produce significant effects.

Risks: LSD may trigger psychosis or long-lasting mental health conditions, particularly in individuals with a predisposition to such disorders. High doses can lead to panic attacks, severe anxiety, and dangerous behavior due to impaired judgment and perception. Chronic use can result in persistent psychological issues, and users may develop tolerance, requiring higher doses to achieve the same effects, which increases the risk of adverse reactions.

Punishments

Penalties for drug offenses depend on the type and quantity involved. Severe cases may lead to imprisonment or fines as follows:

  • Cannabis: Possession of over 100 grams results in up to 15 years imprisonment.
  • Cocaine: Possession of over 10 grams can lead to 20 years imprisonment.
  • Heroin: Severe cases may result in life imprisonment.

Money Laundering

Money laundering is the process of disguising illegally obtained funds to make them appear legitimate. Criminals engage in money laundering to conceal the origins of their illicit gains, evade detection, and integrate the funds into the legitimate financial system. The Drug Enforcement Commission (DEC) in Zambia plays a crucial role in investigating and preventing money laundering to protect the economy and maintain financial integrity.

Common Techniques

  • Layering Financial Transactions: This involves conducting a series of complex financial transactions to obscure the origins of the illicit funds. By moving money through multiple accounts, banks, and jurisdictions, criminals make it difficult for authorities to trace the money back to its illegal source.
  • Using Shell Companies: Shell companies are entities without active business operations or significant assets. Criminals use these companies to hide ownership and control of illicit funds. By routing money through a network of shell companies, they create a web of transactions that is challenging for authorities to untangle.
  • Smuggling Goods and Laundering Proceeds Through Trade: Trade-based money laundering involves manipulating trade transactions to disguise the movement of illicit funds. This can include over-invoicing or under-invoicing goods and services, allowing criminals to transfer value across borders under the guise of legitimate trade.

Role of the DEC

The DEC is responsible for investigating and combating money laundering in Zambia. Established in 1989, the commission operates under the Prohibition and Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2001. The DEC collaborates with other law enforcement agencies, financial institutions, and international organizations to detect and prevent money laundering activities. Key functions of the DEC include:

  • Investigations: The DEC conducts thorough investigations into suspected money laundering activities. This involves gathering evidence, tracing financial transactions, and identifying the individuals and entities involved.
  • Enforcement: The DEC enforces anti-money laundering laws by prosecuting offenders and seizing assets obtained through illegal activities. This helps to disrupt criminal networks and deter future money laundering attempts.
  • Collaboration: The DEC works closely with other agencies, such as the Zambia Revenue Authority (ZRA), to share information and coordinate efforts in combating money laundering and related crimes.
  • Education and Awareness: The DEC conducts public awareness campaigns to educate the public and financial institutions about the risks and signs of money laundering. This helps to build a more vigilant and informed society.

Effects of Money Laundering

Money laundering has severe economic and social consequences. It undermines the integrity of financial institutions, promotes crime and corruption, and destabilizes economies. Some of the key effects include:

  • Economic Instability: Money laundering can lead to unpredictable changes in money demand, causing fluctuations in international capital flows and exchange rates. This can disrupt monetary policy and economic stability.
  • Reduced Foreign Investment: Countries with high levels of money laundering are less attractive to foreign investors, leading to reduced economic growth and investment opportunities.
  • Increased Crime and Corruption: Money laundering provides financial resources for criminal activities, fueling organized crime, drug trafficking, and terrorism. It also facilitates corruption by enabling criminals to bribe officials and evade detection.
  • Damage to Financial Institutions: Money laundering erodes public trust in financial institutions, leading to reduced confidence and stability in the financial system.

Reasons Criminals Engage in Money Laundering

Criminals engage in money laundering for several reasons:

  • Conceal Illicit Activities: Money laundering allows criminals to hide the illegal activities that generated the funds, such as drug trafficking, corruption, and fraud.
  • Evade Detection and Confiscation: By making the money appear legitimate, criminals reduce the risk of being caught and having their assets seized by authorities.
  • Facilitate Financial Transactions: Laundered money can be used for personal or criminal purposes, such as acquiring assets, funding further criminal activities, or expanding illegal operations.
  • Maintain a Reputable Image: Money laundering enables criminals to engage in legitimate investments and business activities without arousing suspicion.
  • Circumvent Financial Controls: By using complex financial structures and offshore accounts, criminals can bypass financial regulations and controls designed to detect illicit activities.

By understanding the techniques, effects, and motivations behind money laundering, we can better appreciate the importance of the DEC's efforts in combating this pervasive crime and protecting Zambia's economy.
